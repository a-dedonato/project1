Q: What is improv anyway?

A: Improv is a form of live theatrical performance that is entirely made up on the spot. While the performers may get suggestions from and interact with the audience, the large majority of what's said and done on-stage has never been done before and will never be done again.

Q: What's the difference between "short-form" and "long-form" improv?

A: "Short-form" improv, also known as "game-based" improv focuses primarily on completing short exercises that give the improvisers rules, restrictions, and objectives and each stand alone as 3-4 minute pieces. In "long-form" improv, or "scene-based" improv, the performers are afforded much more freedom and focus primarily on the interactions and relationships between the characters. If these performances have any rules or structure, they are typically intended to bring the scenes together into a cohesive show rather than being related to the scenes themselves.

Q: What are the origins of improv?

A: Actors have used improvisation exercises to improve their acting ability or to warm up before a performance for at least the last century. It was the Compass Players, an offshoot of The University of Chicago's theater program, that developed these exercises into an artform that could stand on its own through the 1950s, '60s, and '70s.
